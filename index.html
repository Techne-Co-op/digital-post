00 · Overview

A post office
the public owns.

The Digital Post is a cooperatively governed network for reliable digital communication and verified service delivery — a civic institution designed from the ground up to be owned and operated by the communities it serves.

"Everyone has the right to freedom of opinion and expression; this right includes freedom to hold opinions without interference and to seek, receive and impart information and ideas through any media and regardless of frontiers."

Universal Declaration of Human Rights · Article 19 · 1948

The right to communicate is foundational. Every modern institution that honors it — the postal service, the telephone network, the public library — was built as a commons, not a product. The internet collapsed that tradition. We propose restoring it.

The first node exists in Boulder, Colorado.
RegenHub, LCA — a Colorado Limited Cooperative Association — is the legal vessel. The network is designed to federate: each additional city becomes a cooperative node, governed locally, connected globally. The stamp you use here works anywhere the protocol runs.

What the Digital Post is not

It is not a messaging app. It is not a blockchain project. It is not a startup looking for an exit. It is the substrate that messaging apps, public record systems, and community services run on — owned by the people who depend on it, operated as a public utility, accountable to the communities it serves.

The postal stamp is the right metaphor: prepaid, stable in denomination, accepted wherever the network runs, redeemable for real delivered service. The stamp doesn't expire. The network doesn't sell your data. The surplus goes back to the members.

01 · Lineage

Not a new idea.
An old one, carried forward.

Every reliable communication network in history was built as a commons. We are recovering a tradition, not inventing one.

1840
Penny Post, United Kingdom. Rowland Hill's reform unified postal rates across Britain at one penny per half-ounce, regardless of distance. The prepaid stamp was born — a credit instrument backed by guaranteed service delivery. Universal access to correspondence became law.
1874
Universal Postal Union, Berne. Twenty-two nations agreed to treat each other's letters as domestic mail. A federated protocol, not a single monopoly. The stamp in your pocket worked across borders because the nodes had agreed on interoperability.
1936
Rural Electrification Act, United States. Electricity was not reaching farms and small towns — the market deemed it unprofitable. The federal government created cooperatives to extend the grid. Within two decades, 90% of rural homes had power. Public infrastructure works when it is structured as a public good.
1948
UDHR Article 19. In the aftermath of fascism, the international community declared the right to communicate — to seek, receive, and impart information — as a fundamental human right. Every communication infrastructure built since exists against this standard.
1969
ARPANET. The internet was invented as a resilient, decentralized communication network funded by the public. It was designed to route around damage — including the damage of centralized control. The original architecture was a commons. What happened next was not.
2026
The Digital Post, Boulder, Colorado. A cooperatively owned network for reliable digital communication and verified service delivery. The stamp still works. The obligation still holds. The question is who owns the infrastructure underneath it.
The stamp predates email by 140 years.
It survived every technology transition because it encodes something deeper than a delivery mechanism: the public expectation that communication infrastructure is reliable, affordable, and available to all. We are not proposing something new. We are proposing something durable.
02 · The Stamp

Prepaid. Stable. Yours.

The stamp is a prepaid credit for real delivered service. It maps directly from the postal tradition to the four primitives of modern digital infrastructure.

Postal concept Digital primitive Unit
Postage stamp Compute credit compute-hour
Delivery route Data transfer GB transferred
P.O. Box Short-term memory GB-hour
Archive Long-term memory GB-month

One stamp. Four primitives. Every digital service reduces to some combination of these four things — computing, moving, holding briefly, holding long. The stamp is a credit that can be redeemed for any of them, in any proportion, at any node on the network.

How it works

Fixed issuance price
The price to acquire a stamp is set and published. You always know what you're paying. No surge pricing, no dynamic fees, no dark patterns.
Variable redemption rate
What a stamp redeems for adjusts quarterly based on actual infrastructure costs. If compute gets cheaper, your stamp buys more. Rate changes are published in advance and voted on by members.
No expiration
The Forever Stamp mechanic: a stamp purchased today is valid in perpetuity. The USPS Forever Stamp has held purchasing power for decades. So will ours.
Closed-loop credit
Stamps are redeemable for services on the network, not convertible to cash. A service credit, not a financial instrument — the same structure as a transit pass or a library card.

Financial services on the same rails

The postal tradition included money orders, savings accounts, and remittance — basic financial services for people without access to banks. The Digital Post revives this. The same cooperative infrastructure that delivers digital services can offer member savings, community lending, and low-cost transfers — all governed by the same board, held to the same cooperative principles.

Is the stamp a security?
No. The stamp is designed to fall outside the Howey test on all four prongs — there is no investment of money into a common enterprise with an expectation of profit from the efforts of others. It is a prepaid service credit, not a speculative instrument. The full legal analysis is in the Architecture section.
03 · Architecture

The function
without the form.

The Digital Post is a protocol, not a product. The legal and technical architecture is designed to be adopted by any cooperative, in any jurisdiction, while remaining locally governed.

Five-layer stack

L1 Constitution Operating principles and governance rules. Ratified by member vote. Sets the bounds within which all other layers operate.
L2 Protocol The technical standard for how nodes exchange services and credit. Open source. Auditable. Not owned by any single entity.
L3 Attestation Verified service delivery. Nodes attest that services were actually rendered before credit is transferred. No delivery, no payment.
L4 Nodes Cooperative operators who run infrastructure and serve members. Each node is locally governed, globally connected via the protocol.
L5 Credit The stamp. Issued at fixed price, redeemed for services at nodes, never speculative, never expiring. The visible surface of the network.

Four-entity legal structure

The network is held by four interlocking legal entities, each serving a distinct function. Together they provide cooperative ownership, long-term stewardship, financial services capability, and member protection.

Operator
Colorado LCA
Limited Cooperative Association under C.R.S. 7-58-101. Patron members hold ≥51% of voting power. Public benefit purpose. This is the vessel that owns and operates the network.
Steward
Purpose Trust
Colorado Statutory Trust under C.R.S. 38-44-201. Holds the protocol and operating principles in trust for the network's public benefit purpose. Funded by 0.5% of gross revenue.
Financial Services
Licensed MSB
Money Services Business licensed under Colorado's Uniform Money Services Act. Enables member savings, transfers, and financial services on cooperative rails.
Protection
Guarantee Fund
Member protection reserve. If a node fails or service is not delivered, the Guarantee Fund makes members whole. Minimum reserve ratios set in the Operating Agreement.

Colorado statutory grounding

C.R.S. 7-58-101 Colorado Limited Cooperative Association Act. Authorizes the LCA form — cooperative with flexible patron/investor member governance.
C.R.S. 7-58-701 Patron members must hold ≥51% of voting power. Ensures cooperative control cannot be diluted by investment capital.
C.R.S. 7-101-503 Public benefit purpose registration. "Cultivating scenius" is the stated public benefit — the collective intelligence of a community in a place.
C.R.S. 11-51-308.5 Colorado Digital Token Act. Exempts tokens that are not marketed as investments and function solely as access or service credits from securities registration.
C.R.S. 11-110-101 Money Transmitters Act. Closed-loop service credits redeemable only within the network are exempt from money transmitter licensing requirements.
C.R.S. 38-44-201 Colorado Uniform Statutory Trust Entity Act. Enables the Purpose Trust that holds the protocol in long-term stewardship.

Howey analysis

The Howey test determines whether an instrument is a security under U.S. federal law. The stamp is designed to fail all four prongs.

Investment of money Not met Members purchase a prepaid service credit, not an investment. The intent is consumption of services, not capital appreciation.
Common enterprise Not met Stamp value is not tied to the fortunes of the cooperative. The redemption rate adjusts by cost, not by profit.
Expectation of profit Not met Stamps are marketed and designed for service consumption. They do not appreciate, do not pay dividends, and are not convertible to cash.
Efforts of others Not met Value is delivered through service, not through managerial skill or third-party efforts. The stamp buys compute, not a stake in the operator's business.
04 · The Building

Where it meets
the ground.

A network without a place is a network without accountability. The first node is a building — a physical address in Boulder where the cooperative's work is visible, accessible, and verifiable.

The building is structured as a civic commons. Each floor serves a distinct function, and each function is open to the community it serves. The infrastructure lives in the building; so does the governance; so does the public benefit work.

RF
Observatory
Outlook · Cultivation · Sky
Rooftop gathering, sensor infrastructure, sky observation. Where the network touches the atmosphere — ecological sensing, weather, air quality. The building sees its watershed.
L3
Forum
Deliberation · Research · Stillness
Member governance, study rooms, quiet work. Board meetings, member votes, working groups. Research that informs cooperative decisions.
L2
Workshop
Making · Learning · Repair
Fabrication, tool library, teaching space. The cooperative's capacity to make things, fix things, and teach others. Skills circulate here. The Workshop runs on stamps.
L1
Commons
Service · Gathering · Gallery
Street-level. Public access. Member services desk, community gathering space, rotating exhibition. The network's public face — where anyone can walk in and ask what this is.

Growth arc

The network grows by federation, not acquisition. Each new city is a new cooperative — locally owned, locally governed, connected by the protocol. The building program scales with the nodes.

Year 1
Boulder, Colorado
First node operational. Founding members. Physical building. Protocol v1. The stamp is live. Governance is tested in the real.
Years 2–5
Front Range Corridor
Denver, Fort Collins, Colorado Springs. Three to five cooperative nodes. The stamp works across the corridor. Shared infrastructure lowers per-node cost.
Years 5–10
Colorado Statewide
Rural nodes, municipal partnerships. The cooperative model demonstrates viability for underserved communities. The Purpose Trust begins receiving its 0.5% assessment.
Years 10–20
Mountain West
New Mexico, Utah, Wyoming, Montana. The protocol crosses state lines. Node cooperatives are independent legal entities, connected by protocol, held to the same constitutional principles.
Year 20+
Federated Network
The Digital Post becomes what the Universal Postal Union was in 1874: a federated network of cooperatively governed nodes, interoperable by protocol, accountable to the communities they serve.
05 · Applications

What runs
on the network.

Verified service delivery opens civic applications that are impossible on commercial infrastructure. Six categories, drawn from the Ethereum Foundation's Verifiable Cities framework.

Programmable Policy compute
Rules that execute when conditions are met. Permits that issue automatically when inspections pass. Benefits that disburse when eligibility is verified. Policy as code, enforced without discretion.
Outcomes-Based Contracts attestation
Service providers are paid when outcomes are verified, not when invoices are submitted. The attestation layer makes payment contingent on delivery — at the protocol level.
Privacy-Preserving IDs memory
Prove what you need to prove without revealing what you don't. Age verification without date of birth. Residency without address. The minimum disclosure principle, implemented in protocol.
Cross-Agency Verification transfer
Credentials that move between agencies without centralized databases. A school district can verify enrollment for transit benefits without sharing student records with the transit authority.
Verifiable Credentials attestation
Licenses, certifications, and qualifications that people own and control. A contractor's license that lives in the contractor's hands, not in a government database that can be revoked without notice.
Public Works Visibility compute · transfer
Infrastructure spending that is verifiable in real time. Road repair timelines, utility work, park maintenance — visible to any member with a stamp, provable against attested service delivery.
The network is the credential.
Every service delivered through The Digital Post is attested. The attestation record is the proof of delivery. This makes the network inherently a public works visibility system — not as a separate application, but as a property of the infrastructure itself. You don't need a special reporting module. You need the protocol running honestly.
